# 'Betrayed' by burgers?

Lots of people enjoy hamburgers. But recently, customers noticed that hamburgers from popular fast-food brands like McDonald’s and KFC are getting smaller.

Many online users posted pictures as proof, comparing hamburgers with AirPods Pro, soda cup lids or even ketchup packets to show the obvious size difference. Some bloggers even weighed the burgers, finding that a McDonald’s Double Cheeseburger weighs only about 100 grams, much smaller than people remembered.

The smaller burgers come with higher prices. McDonald’s raised most product prices by 0.5 to 1 yuan in late 2025, the second increase that year and the third consecutive year-end adjustment, according to Vista Magazine, a popular Chinese news weekly. KFC also raised takeout prices by an average of 0.8 yuan in January.

Many consumers and fans feel betrayed. **For years, KFC and McDonald’s have built their image as a reliable eating option for workers.** KFC launched the Crazy Thursday meal in 2018, sparking a surge of memes and wide sharing known as Crazy Thursday Literature. McDonald’s followed up with the over-10-yuan “1 + 1 Combo” meal in 2019.

# ‘6-7’ made special

Have you ever heard of "6-7"? Named Dictionary.com's 2025 Word of the Year, the phrase doesn't have a specific meaning. Some suggest it means "so-so" or "maybe this, maybe that", especially when accompanied by a hand gesture where both palms face up and alternate moving up and down.

However, the term has moved beyond the dictionary and into the world of basketball. Young fans and players watch closely as teams get close to 67 points, and excitement erupts when their team hits this mark.

The Charlotte Hornets, an NBA team, are part of this trend. After rapper Skrilla's song *Doot Doot (6 7)* dropped in late 2024, TikTok creators used it in basketball highlight edits, especially for **6-foot-7** players like LaMelo Ball. Taking advantage of the online popularity, the Hornets introduced "6-7" cams during time-outs at some games, highlighting players who are **6 feet 7** inches tall. "For us as a franchise, we always want to listen to our fans. Sometimes you do that by observing what they're into – their trends and likes. It's a way for us to connect with them...," Seth Bennett, the Hornets' chief marketing officer, told AP News.

> 1. 为什么一个是“6-foot-7”，还有一个是“6 feet 7”？从“为什么有无连词符”和“为什么 foot 的单复数不一样”两个要点说明。

The "6-7" trend is particularly clear when games are on the 67-point watch. Daniel Durbin, director of USC Annenberg Institute of Sports, Media and Society, got a direct experience when he attended a basketball game on Feb 1. When a player made a free throw, bringing the score to 67, the crowd erupted in cheers.

Durbin noted that this phenomenon is part of a long history of arbitrary sports traditions that fans create to feel more caught up in the fun. "**Think of all the superstitions fans have during games, rituals they enact to 'help' the team win**," he told AP News. "As fans walk to USC football games, most of them kick the base of a certain lamppost. Why? It makes them part of the game. They are enacting a meaningless ritual for luck."

# Laser leap for China

Transmitting information from 40,000 kilometers above Earth might sound like the stuff of science fiction, but Chinese scientists have turned it into reality. They've successfully maintained a high-speed laser link with a high-orbit satellite for over three hours.

According to Xinhua, China has launched multiple high-orbit and low-Earth orbit satellites to provide internet services. High-orbit satellites are typically 36,000 kilometers above Earth, while LEO satellites range from a few hundred kilometers to 2,000 kilometers. Due to their higher altitude, high-orbit satellites can cover larger areas. However, the high altitude can bring problems. The communication between these satellites and the ground isn't stable, lasting only minutes. Also, while data travels quickly from satellites, signals from Earth are much slower. "It's like sending someone 10 messages and getting only one reply," Liu Chao, a researcher from the Chinese Academy of Sciences, told Sichuan Daily. "It's hard to have an efficient conversation that way."

To solve these problems, the research team developed a laser communication ground station with a 1.8-meter-wide optical telescope in Lijiang, Yunnan province. Acting like a sensitive eye, the station can capture weak signals from over 40,000 kilometers away. Additionally, scientists used a guiding laser to help the satellite and ground station quickly find each other. They also employed precise tracking and real-time corrections to keep the "conversation" stable, reported Sichuan Daily.

The efforts have resulted in a breakthrough. At distances of up to 40,740 kilometers, the ground station and a high-orbit satellite achieved two-way communication in just four seconds. **The link, which is as fast as one gigabit per second, lasted over three hours uninterrupted.**

This lays a foundation for upgrading high-orbit satellites from data relay stations into intelligent processing centers in our space network. The experiment also proved the deep-space communication abilities of ground stations. "With this technology, we will be able to communicate with deep space probes on the moon, Mars and other locations directly from Earth," Liu told Sichuan Daily.

# Challenges in friendship

I vividly recall my first day at the University of British Columbia. I was 17, eagerly anticipating college life. Everyone said these would be the best years of my life – the golden ticket to finding lifelong friends.

Making friends had never been a challenge for me, so I had no doubts. But reality hit quickly. During Jump Start, I hid in my room for three days as I was too embarrassed to eat alone in the dining hall. I was completely trapped by the fear of being judged, terrified of looking like I didn't belong.

Things turned when I met an Instagram friend who introduced me to his roommate and a few friends. Soon, we became a small group and I thought I had found forever friends. We did all the typical first-year stuff – going to classes, sharing meals and studying together. But gradually, a subtle shift occurred. No major incidents, but I couldn't ignore my growing discomfort. I started dreading our meetups, wrestling with social anxiety at every gathering. Each interaction became more challenging.

Later I realized that friendships require a solid foundation. A lack of common interests, shared goals and values made it harder to connect with them. The group eventually split. Even though I'd felt uneasy for months, the breakup broke my heart. In university, losing friends hits differently – it's easy to believe you'll be alone forever.

Living in an era dominated by social media didn't make situations like this any easier. It's too easy to compare your life to others'. Seeing others out with friends felt like a personal failure. Everyone else seemed to have found their place while I struggled with the simple task of making friends.

After the group split, two friends from the group stayed in touch. Tough times reveal true friends. Though we weren't particularly close before, they stuck by me at my lowest. We may not be friends forever, but they really make every day brighter.

Now in my second year, my perspective on friendship has shifted significantly. I want to tell any first-year struggling: Sometimes, finding your people just takes a little time, patience and a little heartbreak along the way.

— By Kaasvi Bhatia
